Privacy Policy

Yearworm · last updated 18 July 2026
Short version: Yearworm has no ads, no cookies, and no cross-site trackers, and works fully without an account. If you opt in to the social features, we store the minimum they need: a nickname you choose, scores, your friends list — and only if you link Google, your Google ID and email so your account survives a new phone. Everything else stays on your own device.

What we collect

Nothing, unless you opt in. Playing Yearworm requires no account and no sign-in, and we use no advertising or cross-site tracking of any kind. The optional online features (leaderboard, player profiles, Google sign-in) store only what is described below, and each one is off until you use it.

Anonymous visit statistics

To see whether anyone plays the game at all, Yearworm uses GoatCounter, a privacy-friendly analytics service. It uses no cookies and stores no personal information — we only see aggregate numbers such as "how many visits today", which page, the referring site, country, and coarse events like "a daily challenge was started". None of it can be traced back to you, and it is never shared or used for advertising.

Data stored on your device

To let you resume a game and keep your settings, the app saves a few things in your browser's local storage (on your device only): your current game state, any custom decks you build, difficulty preference, and personal-best scores. This data never leaves your device and is not visible to us. You can clear it any time by clearing the app's site data.

Daily leaderboard

If you play the daily challenge, your result is submitted to our leaderboard server so you can see your rank. What is sent: a nickname you choose (shown publicly on the leaderboard — pick anything, it doesn't have to identify you), your score and completion time, and a random token generated on your device (so the board can recognise your own entry — it says nothing about you or your hardware). No account, no email, no IP is stored. The leaderboard runs on Cloudflare infrastructure.

Player profiles & Google sign-in (optional)

You can optionally claim a player name to add friends and challenge them directly. That stores your chosen name, a random friend code, your friends list, and challenges you send or receive — tied to a random token, not to your identity.

You can additionally link a Google account so your profile survives switching phones. If you do, we store your Google account's stable ID and its email address (used only to recognise your account and for support — never for marketing, never shared). Signing in shows you Google's own consent screen; their handling of the sign-in is governed by Google's privacy policy. To delete your profile and any linked data, email us at the address below and it will be removed.

If you turn on notifications, your browser gives us a push subscription (an anonymous delivery address for your device) which we store so we can alert you when a friend challenges you, adds you, or plays your challenge, plus your device's timezone offset so an optional evening reminder about your daily streak arrives at a sensible hour. It holds no personal information and is used only to deliver these notifications; turning notifications off in the app (or your browser settings) removes it.

Voluntary correction reports

If you correct a song's release year, you can optionally tap a button to report that correction to us so we can fix it for everyone. Such a report contains only the song title, artist, the old and new year, and the app version — nothing about you or your device.

Third-party services

Playing the game makes ordinary web requests to these external services:

  • Apple iTunes — to search for songs and stream their 30-second preview clips.
  • Google Fonts — to load the app's typography.
  • GoatCounter — the anonymous visit statistics described above.
  • Cloudflare — our own leaderboard/profile server (described above) runs on Cloudflare infrastructure and is contacted when the app checks for challenges or scores.
  • Google Sign-In — the sign-in button's script loads from Google; actual sign-in only happens if you tap it.

As with any website, these services receive the standard technical information a browser sends with a request (such as your IP address). Yearworm sends them no personal data of its own. Their handling of such requests is governed by their own privacy policies.

Children

Yearworm is a general-audience music game and does not knowingly collect personal data from children; the optional profile features store only a self-chosen nickname unless a Google account is deliberately linked.
